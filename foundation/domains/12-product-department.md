# Domain 12: Product Department Context

## The Department and Its Place in the Company

The Product Department occupies a unique and strategically critical position inside Memphis Tours. It sits at the intersection of market intelligence, commercial design, and operational planning — the place where raw opportunity is transformed into something a traveler from Milan or São Paulo or New York can actually book. In a company of 348 people operating 16 brands across 8+ destinations in 10 languages, the Product Department is small. Ziad Farrag, the Director of Planning, leads a team of approximately six to eight people. His closest collaborator is Bassem Saad, the Product Manager, who carries primary responsibility for day-to-day catalog management, itinerary construction, and the mechanics of keeping roughly 17,000 packages current and commercially viable. Beneath them sit two or three Product Analysts, whose job is the intelligence function — reading demand, monitoring competitors, tracking performance. One or two Content Coordinators handle the cross-brand, cross-language consistency challenge, ensuring that a product sold through memphistours.it carries the same accuracy and voice as the version presented on the German site or the TravelVerse global brand. A single Pricing Specialist manages the cost architecture: supplier quotes, markup structures, seasonal rate calendars, and the currency considerations that come with selling in USD, EUR, GBP, and BRL simultaneously.

What makes this team's influence disproportionate to its size is a simple fact: every product sold by every one of Memphis Tours' sixteen brands — from the main US site to niche properties like She Drives, GoElite, and Egypt and Jordan Tours — passes through the decisions this department makes. The Product Department does not close bookings or run operations. But it decides what exists to be sold. That makes it the commercial foundation on which everything else rests.

## How Products Are Actually Built Today

The journey from market opportunity to live product is part formal process, part craft, and part institutional instinct accumulated over decades of operating in the Egypt and MENA travel space. Understanding how it works today is essential context for understanding what the Product Engine is designed to change.

The process begins with what the team calls signal detection, though that phrase overstates its current formality. In practice, a signal is usually a message from the Italian sales team saying clients keep asking for a particular Turkey itinerary, or Ziad noticing a pattern in competitor activity, or the Latin America team flagging rising demand for multi-country combinations. There is no systematic monitoring layer — no dashboard that surfaces emerging demand, no automated alert when a destination's inquiry volume shifts. The signal arrives as a conversation, an email, or a hunch. This means opportunities are identified when someone happens to notice them, not when the data first makes them visible.

Once a signal is recognized, the team moves to research. This is perhaps the most labor-intensive step in the current workflow and the one with the greatest inconsistency. The Product Analysts browse competitor websites manually — checking what Intrepid, G Adventures, On The Go Tours, and local competitors like Egypt Best Vacations or Ramasside Tours are offering. They call suppliers to probe availability and pricing. They review the CRM system, system_travelapp, for historical demand data on similar products or destination patterns. It works, but it is slow, and its quality depends entirely on the bandwidth and rigor of whoever is running the research on any given week. There is no audit trail, no consistent methodology, and no way to accumulate these findings into a growing institutional knowledge base.

With research in hand, Bassem Saad takes the lead on design. Designing an itinerary is not simply assembling components — it requires deep knowledge of route logic, supplier reliability, accommodation quality at different price points, pacing for different source markets (Italian travelers tend to prefer a different rhythm than Brazilian or American ones), and the kind of experiential texture that turns an adequate tour into a memorable one. This work happens in spreadsheets and Word documents. It is skilled craft work, and the output quality is high. But it is poorly documented and difficult to scale or hand off, because so much of the logic lives in Bassem's and Ziad's experience rather than in the artifacts they produce.

Costing is handled by the Pricing Specialist in Excel. The model covers supplier quotes at different volumes, the markup calculation across product tiers, seasonal price variation across the calendar, and currency effects for major source markets. It is functional but fragile — a change in a supplier rate requires manual updates cascading across multiple files, and there is no live link between the cost model and the live booking system.

Once pricing is settled, the content team takes over. Product descriptions are written in English first and then adapted — not merely translated, but localized — for each language brand. This localization carries real commercial weight: what resonates with a French traveler is not identical to what compels a Brazilian one, and the Content Coordinators navigate those differences while keeping factual and operational details consistent. Photos are sourced. The cross-brand accuracy check is performed. This step is where the sixteen-brand reality becomes most operationally demanding.

Finally, the product is entered into the tr_packages system and activated on the relevant brand websites. That is the launch. Post-launch, performance is monitored periodically — but there is no systematic health tracking. Products that begin underperforming often go undetected for months. A Nile cruise package with a declining conversion rate, a Turkey itinerary that is generating inquiries but not converting, a Morocco product that stopped selling in Brazil but continues to sell well in Italy — these patterns exist in the data, but nothing surfaces them automatically. The team finds out when the problem is already significant.

## The Tools on the Desk

The team's current toolset reflects a smart, pragmatic group working within the constraints of what is available rather than what would be ideal. Excel and Google Sheets form the backbone of pricing and market analysis. Word and Google Docs hold itinerary documents and proposals. The CRM — the system_travelapp database at the heart of Memphis Tours' operations — is used to view booking data and run basic reports, though extracting meaningful insight from its 518 tables and nearly 850,000 request records requires either manual SQL queries or a request to Abdelrahman Zarif's Analytics team. ChatGPT has become an informal but increasingly important tool for content drafting, translation assistance, and quick market research sketches. Google Analytics provides website traffic context. TripAdvisor serves double duty: monitoring Memphis Tours' own review performance (15,000+ reviews at a 4.9 average) and benchmarking against competitor sentiment. And email remains the primary channel for supplier coordination — no integrated supplier portal, no systematic communication log.

This toolkit is not inadequate for a team of this size. But it creates a fundamental mismatch: the team is sitting on one of the most valuable commercial intelligence assets in regional travel — 849,000 requests, 866,000 clients, 303,000 enquiries, 520,000 service orders accumulated since 2010 — and has no systematic way to interrogate it. The data knows things the team does not yet know it knows.

## What Frustrates the Team Most

The frustrations inside the Product Department are concrete, specific, and shared across the team in ways that surface regularly in planning conversations.

The deepest frustration is the intelligence gap. The database contains enough signal to answer almost any commercial question the team needs answered: Which destinations are trending with Italian clients this quarter? What is the conversion rate differential between private tours and small group tours in the Brazilian market? Which products have the highest review scores and what do they have in common? What is the typical revenue per client for a Turkey package booked through the German site versus the US site? These questions are not exotic. They are the exact questions that should drive product strategy. And they cannot be answered without filing a request with the Analytics team or building a custom SQL query, which means they usually are not answered at all. The team makes decisions with incomplete information not from lack of data, but from lack of access to the insight embedded in the data.

The second frustration is the reactive posture the team is locked into. New products get built because someone raised their hand and said demand is here. Products get retired when the failure is already obvious. The department wants to be predictive — to see the demand curve rising before it peaks, to identify the competitive gap before a rival fills it. Currently, the structure does not support that ambition. There is no system watching the signals and surfacing opportunities before they become obvious to everyone.

The third frustration is cross-brand consistency at scale. Sixteen brands in six-plus languages means that a seemingly simple change — a hotel partner swap in a Jordan itinerary, a price adjustment for the Turkish season, an updated departure frequency — triggers a cascade of updates that must be made accurately across all relevant brand presentations. One missed update creates a pricing discrepancy. One untranslated change creates a content inconsistency. The Content Coordinators manage this with significant effort, but it is error-prone and does not scale as the brand portfolio grows.

## How the Department Makes Decisions

Within the existing destination portfolio, the Product Manager can initiate and design new products with Director approval from Ziad. This keeps the team agile on familiar ground — adding a new Egypt private tour variant or extending a Jordan itinerary does not require escalation. Entering a new destination, or making a significant change to a flagship product, requires Director-plus-Senior-Management approval, drawing in Mohamed Hassan and Mohamed Amer. Major product retirements involve Sales leadership — because removing a product affects what the sales teams can offer, particularly in the language markets where that product was a consistent earner. Decisions about entirely new country entries rise to the executive level.

This decision structure means the Product Department has real autonomy in its core domain while ensuring that consequential decisions are made with appropriate cross-functional input. The constraint is not the governance model — it is the quality of information available when those decisions are made.

## The Network of Dependencies

Product decisions ripple across almost every other department. The Sales teams — English under Marwa Aaziz, Latin under Sherif Gamal, Dubai under Yosri AbdAllah, and the Italian, French, German, and Spanish market teams — are simultaneously the Product Department's primary customer and its most important intelligence source. Sales provides the unfiltered demand signal from the front lines; Product provides the catalog that Sales is able to monetize. This relationship works best when it flows both directions in real time, and breaks down when either side operates in isolation.

Operations, under Hassan Musleh, is the feasibility gate. A product that cannot be executed reliably at the promised quality level should not exist. Operations confirms supplier availability, guide capacity, ground logistics, and the operational nuances that can make or break a guest experience. The dialogue between Product and Operations is especially important when designing products for newer destinations like Mexico or for emerging niches like accessible travel or women-only tours.

Marketing — Mahmoud Albanna on the brand and strategy side, Mai Mohamed on eMarketing — needs Product's catalog to build campaigns, and needs accurate content and imagery to do it effectively. In the other direction, Product needs Marketing's view on what is generating interest, what search volumes look like for different destination categories, and what the TripAdvisor and review landscape says about sentiment.

Finance, under Mahmoud Ewida, provides the margin analysis that validates whether a proposed product is commercially viable, and approves the pricing structure before launch. The relationship with Abdelrahman Zarif's Analytics team is perhaps the most strategically significant dependency: it is Analytics that holds the data access and query capability that Product needs to answer its most important questions. And Reservation, led by Abdallah Bihiry, feeds back the operational ground truth — when a product is generating booking complications, last-minute supplier issues, or client complaints at the point of service, Reservation is where that signal first appears.

## What the Team Would Do With Unlimited Capacity

If the Product Department had the research capacity to match its ambition, it would systematically profile every source market against every destination in its portfolio. It would run continuous competitive benchmarks, not occasional manual checks. It would monitor product health in real time — conversion rate, average booking value, review sentiment, repeat client rate — and surface early warning signs months before they become problems. It would have a prioritized pipeline of product opportunities, ranked by demand signal strength and competitive gap analysis, so that when a sales team asks "can we have more Turkey products for the Italian market," the answer is already prepared.

The team's most honest articulation of this aspiration is captured in the question they describe as the most-asked unanswerable: "Which product should we build next?" Today, that question is answered by experience, instinct, and whoever happened to raise their hand most recently. The Product Engine is designed to change that — to synthesize demand signals from 849,000 historical requests, competitive gap data from across the market, and trend intelligence from the company's own review and booking history into a ranked, reasoned answer. Not to replace Ziad's judgment or Bassem's craft, but to make both of them work from better information than they have ever had before.

That is the north star for everything the Product Engine does in this department's service.
