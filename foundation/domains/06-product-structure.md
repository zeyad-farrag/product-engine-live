# Domain 6: Product Structure & Catalog

## The Catalog in Context

Memphis Tours maintains approximately 17,000 packages in the `tr_packages` database table, a number that sounds enormous until you understand how it is organized. The catalog spans 8 primary destinations — Egypt, Jordan, the United Arab Emirates, Oman, Turkey, Greece, Morocco, and Mexico — but a substantial portion of that count represents language and brand variants of the same core itinerary. A single "8-Day Classic Egypt Tour" may exist as six or more distinct database records, one per language market (English, Spanish, Italian, French, German, Portuguese), with content, pricing presentation, and marketing copy adapted for each audience. Strip away these variants and the number of genuinely distinct itineraries is considerably smaller. This distinction matters enormously for anyone building systems against the catalog: the logical product and the database row are not always the same thing. The `original_package_id` field is the key that ties variants back to their canonical ancestor.

The scale is still impressive. Even accounting for variants, Memphis Tours operates one of the largest proprietary travel product catalogs in the MENA and Mediterranean DMC space — a reflection of 25+ years of continuous product development since the company first went online in 1999 and of a deliberate strategy to serve multiple source markets through multiple brands simultaneously.

## What a Product Actually Is

At its core, a Memphis Tours package is a bundled travel experience designed to remove all logistical friction from the customer's journey. When a traveler books an "8-Day Cairo, Luxor & Aswan with Nile Cruise," they are purchasing a single contract that covers accommodation (hotel nights selected by tier), ground transportation (typically a private car with a dedicated driver, or a shared vehicle for group products), a licensed local guide assigned to the destination, entrance fees to every site on the itinerary, daily breakfast with optional lunch and dinner, internal flights where geography demands them (the Cairo–Luxor–Aswan triangle is the canonical example), a Nile cruise berth for the relevant nights, and airport transfers at both ends of the trip. The customer experiences Egypt as a continuous, curated journey rather than a series of bookings they have stitched together themselves.

What the product deliberately excludes is equally important and is stated clearly in every package listing: international flights to and from Egypt (or whichever destination), travel insurance, personal expenditures, and optional tips for guides and drivers. This scope definition allows Memphis Tours to control quality on everything it can directly manage while remaining price-competitive on the headline number customers compare across providers. The worry-free promise applies inside the itinerary; getting to the destination remains the traveler's responsibility.

## How the Catalog Is Organized

The organizational hierarchy in the system follows a consistent five-level structure: destination, product type, duration, tier, and language variant. Destination sits at the top because Memphis Tours is fundamentally a destination expert — the company's operational credibility is geographic, and customers begin their search by deciding where they want to go. Below destination, product type defines the fundamental nature of the experience: Travel Package (the broadest multi-day category), Nile Cruise, Shore Excursion, Day Trip, Multi-Country Tour, Accessible Tour, Beach Vacation, Dahabiya, and Safari. These are not just marketing labels; they drive operational logic, guide assignment, supplier relationships, and how service orders are structured downstream.

Duration creates the next major branching point. The catalog runs from single-day excursions through 3-day, 5-day, 7-day, 10-day, and 14-day packages, with custom durations available beyond that. For Egypt, the 8-day format has historically been the commercial sweet spot — long enough to cover Cairo, Luxor, and Aswan meaningfully, short enough to fit within a typical two-week vacation window for the European and American markets that dominate the booking base.

Tier — Budget, Classic, Premium, and Luxury — governs hotel selection and, to some extent, transport quality and guide seniority. A Classic Egypt tour and a Luxury Egypt tour might share an identical itinerary route and many of the same sites, but the hotels, the vehicles, and the caliber of guiding experience differ substantially. Pricing differences between tiers can be dramatic: a 5-day Cairo package runs roughly $800–$1,500 at Classic tier and meaningfully higher in Premium or Luxury configurations.

Language variants complete the hierarchy. Because Memphis Tours operates dedicated websites in English (US and UK), French, Spanish, Italian, and German, plus brands like Egyptian Sidekick (Portuguese/Brazilian) and Vamos Viajando (Latin American Spanish), the same core product must exist in localized form for each market. This is not simple translation — it involves recalibrating the narrative emphasis, adjusting the imagery, and sometimes repricing for market expectations. Italian travelers, who represent the single largest source market at over 131,000 clients in the database, receive itinerary content calibrated for Italian cultural expectations around food, pacing, and historical interpretation. Brazilian clients, numbering nearly 97,000, encounter content through Egyptian Sidekick that speaks to Portuguese-language sensibilities.

## The Core Product Categories

Egypt multi-day packages form the largest single category and the commercial engine of the business. The flagship products here are itineraries like the "8-Day Cairo, Luxor & Aswan with Nile Cruise," priced at $1,500–$3,000 per person depending on tier and season, and the "12-Day Complete Egypt Experience," which can reach $5,000 or more in premium configurations. The "5-Day Cairo & Pyramids Highlights" product, priced at $800–$1,500, serves travelers with shorter windows and functions as an important gateway product that sometimes converts to longer repeat visits. All of these exist in both Private format (dedicated car, guide, and flexible timing) and Small Group format (shared transportation, scheduled departures, lower price point).

Nile Cruises occupy a distinct category both experientially and operationally. Standard 5-star Nile cruises run four nights between Luxor and Aswan (or in reverse), offering travelers a floating hotel experience with onboard lectures, sunset deck access, and shore excursions to Edfu, Kom Ombo, and Philae baked into the schedule. Pricing runs from roughly $400 to $3,000 per person, the variance driven primarily by cabin category and season. The Dahabiya segment is a premium sub-category: these traditional Egyptian sailing boats carry 8–20 passengers and offer a more intimate, slower-paced journey that appeals to experienced travelers seeking an alternative to the larger cruise ships. Lake Nasser cruises, which follow the Abu Simbel route south of the High Dam, are the rarest and most exclusive Nile product, appealing to history enthusiasts willing to pay a significant premium for access to Nubian temples that most tourists never reach.

Shore excursions represent a logistically distinct product type built around the constraints of cruise ship itineraries. Travelers on Mediterranean or Red Sea cruises dock at Alexandria, Port Said, Safaga, or Sokhna for periods ranging from 6 to 10 hours, and Memphis Tours has developed a portfolio of tightly-timed excursions designed to deliver maximum experience within that window. The "Pyramids & Sphinx from Alexandria Port" at $165 and "Luxor Temples from Safaga" at $194 are representative examples. Every shore excursion product carries a guaranteed ship-return policy — Memphis Tours assumes responsibility for getting the client back to the dock on time, a commitment that requires precise logistical choreography and direct relationships with port authorities and ship liners.

Day trips and single-day excursions serve both independent travelers using Egypt as a base and guests staying in resort cities who want to explore inland. From Cairo, the core day trip catalog covers the Giza Pyramids and Sphinx, the Egyptian Museum, Coptic Old Cairo, and a day trip to Alexandria. From Luxor, Valley of the Kings and Karnak Temple are the anchor products. From Hurghada, the catalog includes desert safari experiences, snorkeling excursions, and the Mahmya Island beach day. Pricing spans $40 to $300 per person, making these the most accessible entry points in the catalog and an important source of incremental revenue from clients who have already booked accommodation independently.

Multi-country tours represent the highest-value category by average booking size. Egypt combined with Jordan is the most popular pairing, typically running 10–14 days and priced from $3,000 to $5,000+ per person. The Egypt-Turkey combination and Egypt-Morocco itineraries are established sellers. Three-country combinations — Egypt, Jordan, and a European or African addition — can run 18–21 days and reach $8,000 or above at premium tier. These products require coordination across multiple in-country operators and carry higher service complexity, which is why they tend to be routed through senior travel consultants rather than the general inquiry queue.

Specialized products reflect Memphis Tours' deliberate effort to serve niche segments that the mass market ignores. Accessible tours are designed for wheelchair users and travelers with mobility limitations, with hotel selection, transportation, and site access evaluated against accessibility standards. The She Drives brand delivers solo female tours with female guides and carefully curated accommodation. Honeymoon packages add private transfers, room upgrades, and romantic meal setups as a bundled layer on top of standard itineraries. Family tours adjust pacing, reduce walking distances, and incorporate child-friendly activities. Medical tourism programs, particularly for markets like Germany and India, combine treatment logistics with cultural excursions in a highly specialized offering.

## How Products Are Created and Maintained

The product creation process sits with the Product Department, led by Director of Planning Ziad Farrag and Product Manager Bassem Saad. A new product begins with demand signals: inquiry data showing customers asking for a route or configuration that doesn't exist in the catalog, competitive analysis revealing gaps, or external events like the opening of a new archaeological site or the emergence of a destination in social media travel conversation. When signals reach a threshold, Bassem Saad initiates an itinerary design process that specifies the day-by-day route, selects hotels at each tier level, assigns the appropriate guide type per destination, and works through the operational timing. Supplier quotes are then gathered, markup calculations applied by season (high season runs October through April for Egypt; low season May through September), and the pricing table constructed. Content creation follows — day-by-day descriptions, highlights, inclusion/exclusion lists, and photography — across all relevant language variants. The package is then entered into `tr_packages` with all pricing tiers and activated on the appropriate brand sites.

Products are not static. The lifecycle of an active package averages 2–3 years before significant revision is required. Booking conversion data, post-tour customer ratings, and review patterns all feed back into the product team's optimization cycle. A product that sees its inquiry-to-booking conversion rate decline, or that begins generating below-average satisfaction scores, triggers a review. Sometimes the fix is a pricing adjustment — the most common single cause of underperformance is misalignment between the Memphis Tours price and what competitors are offering for a comparable experience. Sometimes the fix is itinerary restructuring to reflect how traveler expectations have evolved. Products are retired when demand falls below the viability threshold or when operational complexity — a supplier relationship breaking down, a route becoming logistically unworkable — makes the product unprofitable to deliver at acceptable quality.

## The Customization Model and Why It Changes Everything

The most important operational reality about the Memphis Tours product catalog is that it functions less as a fixed menu and more as a design vocabulary. Approximately 70% of actual bookings involve meaningful customization from the published itinerary. A traveler might inquire about the "8-Day Classic Egypt Tour," but after a conversation with a consultant, end up booking a 10-day version that skips Alexandria, adds an extra day in Luxor, upgrades the Nile cruise to a Dahabiya, and substitutes one hotel for a preferred property at the same tier. The catalog product was the starting point; the customized itinerary is what actually gets booked and operated.

The database architecture reflects this reality. The `requests` table records both the `original_package_id` (the catalog entry that attracted the client's attention) and the actual customized itinerary as it was ultimately configured and confirmed. This dual-record structure allows the product team to trace demand back to specific catalog entries even when the delivered product diverged substantially from the original. It means that a catalog package with modest direct bookings may still be generating significant revenue through customized derivatives — a fact that a naive revenue-per-package analysis would completely miss.

This customization model is Memphis Tours' core competitive differentiator against both mass-market operators like TUI or Thomas Cook (who sell fixed departures) and online booking platforms (which cannot replicate the consultative conversation). It requires a sales team capable of fluent itinerary design, a supplier network flexible enough to accommodate non-standard configurations, and an operational system robust enough to track what was actually promised to each client rather than just what the catalog said.

## Pricing and Content Architecture

Pricing is per-person, structured across seasonal tiers, with single supplement charges for solo travelers and discounts of 25–50% for children depending on age band. Groups of six or more receive graduated discounts. Prices are published in USD as the primary currency, with EUR, GBP, and BRL used across the European and Latin American brand sites respectively. Dynamic adjustments based on hotel availability mean that a price displayed today may differ from the same product quoted tomorrow if key hotels are moving toward capacity.

Every package in the catalog carries a content package that includes a destination overview, day-by-day itinerary narrative, a structured inclusion/exclusion list, a seasonal pricing table by tier, 8–15 photographs per package, customer reviews linked from the company's TripAdvisor presence (over 15,000 reviews averaging 4.9), and a related/alternative packages section that serves both SEO and cross-sell functions. This content architecture is replicated across language variants, with localization going beyond translation to encompass photography choices, which sites are emphasized in the narrative, and how pricing is framed — all calibrated for the specific market expectations of Italian, Spanish, French, German, and Portuguese-language travelers.

Product success is measured against four indicators: inquiry-to-booking conversion rate, revenue per booking, post-tour customer satisfaction rating, and repeat and referral rate. The last metric is particularly meaningful given that Memphis Tours has built its reputation over seven decades and 866,000 client records — repeat customers represent compounding value that a single-transaction analysis cannot capture.
