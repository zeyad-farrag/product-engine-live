# Domain 10: Pricing & Commercial Policies

## Pricing Structure

Memphis Tours prices almost all of its products on a per-person basis, with the final per-person rate varying across several key dimensions: room occupancy, passenger age, travel season, accommodation tier, and group size. These variables interact continuously, which is why the sales consultation model remains central — the vast majority of packages are customized rather than sold at a fixed listed rate.

Occupancy adjustments are a foundational element of the pricing logic. A solo traveler pays a single supplement, typically ranging from 25% to 40% above the double-occupancy per-person rate, to cover the fixed hotel room cost that cannot be shared. This supplement applies across virtually all multi-day packages and is one of the more friction-generating line items in the sales process. At the other end, a third adult sharing a room (on a rollaway or triple configuration) typically receives a modest discount of 10–15% compared to the double rate, though not all hotels in the portfolio offer genuine triple rooms — in practice, the third person often shares a double bed, limiting how far the discount can go. The She Drives brand, which caters explicitly to women traveling solo or in small groups, partially mitigates the solo supplement problem by building shared group departures where solo travelers slot into a shared room at the standard double rate.

Child pricing follows a tiered model. Children under two years of age travel free or at a nominal administrative charge (typically $20–$50) provided they do not occupy a seat or berth. Children aged two through eleven receive discounts of 25–50% off the adult rate, depending on the product — the higher discounts apply when the child shares a room with two full-paying adults, reducing the net accommodation cost. Children aged twelve and above are generally charged at the adult rate. These policies are applied at the discretion of the sales consultant and can be adjusted for competitive situations, particularly in the large Italian and Brazilian family-travel segments where child pricing is a decisive factor.

Seasonality creates the most significant pricing variation across the portfolio. Egypt's cultural and heritage tours — Pharaonic monuments, Nile cruises, Luxor, Aswan, Cairo — peak in the October-through-April window, when European and North American travelers seek winter sun and favorable temperatures. This window commands a 15–30% premium over summer pricing on comparable packages. Conversely, Red Sea beach destinations (Hurghada, Sharm el-Sheikh) peak in the June-through-September period, aligning with European summer holidays, and pricing reflects that demand. The Jordan and Turkey portfolios follow similar shoulder patterns to Egypt's cultural season. This dual seasonality is a genuine operational advantage: it allows Memphis Tours to balance consultant workload and supplier capacity across the calendar year rather than concentrating revenue into a single season.

Accommodation tier is the other major pricing lever. Most multi-day packages are offered at three tiers: budget (3-star), mid-range (4-star), and premium (5-star). The price differential between tiers can be substantial — on a seven-night Egypt tour, the gap between a 3-star and a 5-star version may be $600–$1,200 per person. The 5-star segment, which includes products under the GoElite luxury brand, commands the widest gross margin in absolute terms while maintaining the overall 20–35% margin percentage range.

Group size discounts activate at six or more travelers and scale with size, reaching up to 15% for very large private groups. Corporate and incentive groups above thirty travelers receive bespoke pricing negotiated directly with operations and finance, bypassing the standard rate card entirely.

The `requests` table in the database tracks both a `price` field and an `initial_price` field on every booking record. This two-field structure is not accidental — it reflects the commercial reality that the price presented in the first consultant response frequently differs from the price at which the booking ultimately closes. The delta between `initial_price` and `price` captures the cumulative effect of negotiations, discounts, itinerary adjustments, and seasonal repricing that occur during the sales cycle. Understanding this gap at a portfolio level is essential for accurate margin analysis, since the initial_price may represent a more optimistic margin assumption than what is actually realized.

## Currency Strategy

Memphis Tours operates in a genuinely multi-currency commercial environment, and the currency strategy is closely linked to the brand and market structure. The US dollar is the primary selling currency for the English-language site (memphistours.com), the Egypt Tours Plus brand, and the Latin American markets — including the very large Brazilian, Mexican, Argentine, and Colombian segments. USD dominates because these markets are accustomed to pricing international travel in dollars, and it simplifies comparison shopping. The UK site (memphistours.co.uk) prices and invoices in GBP, reflecting regulatory and consumer expectations in that market. The French, Italian, German, and Spanish sites price in EUR, as do all other European brands. The Brazilian-specific brand, Egyptian Sidekick, uses BRL as its display and invoicing currency, though USD is retained as the settlement currency for operational ease.

Currency is tracked at the individual request level via the `currency_id` field, enabling market-level revenue reporting in native currencies while allowing consolidated reporting to be performed in USD for management accounts.

FX risk is managed operationally rather than through financial instruments. Memphis Tours does not hedge its currency exposures in the capital markets. Instead, rates built into the selling price are reviewed and updated periodically — typically monthly during stable periods, more frequently when the Egyptian Pound or a major source-market currency moves sharply. The primary FX exposure arises from the structure of the cost base: supplier costs (hotels, guides, transport, domestic flights, entrance fees) are billed primarily in EGP and, to a lesser degree, USD, while a significant portion of revenue is collected in EUR and GBP. When the Egyptian Pound weakens against USD — as occurred significantly through 2022–2023 — supplier costs in USD terms remain relatively stable but EGP-denominated local costs become cheaper in dollar terms, temporarily expanding margins. When EUR or GBP weakens against USD, the revenue collected from European clients shrinks in dollar terms without a corresponding reduction in USD-denominated costs, compressing margins on those sales. This structural exposure is a known risk that finance reviews quarterly as part of margin management.

## Markup Model and Gross Margins

Memphis Tours earns revenue through the markup between the wholesale cost of the underlying components — hotel nights, private transfers, licensed guides, entrance fees, domestic flights, Nile cruise berths, and destination management fees paid to in-country partners — and the selling price charged to the end customer. There is no commission-based model for the core business; Memphis Tours is the merchant of record.

Gross margins vary materially by product type. Standard private multi-day tours in Egypt, which represent the largest revenue category, operate at 20–30% gross margin. Luxury and premium products, including GoElite packages and Dahabiya Nile cruises, tend to achieve 25–35% gross margin in percentage terms, with significantly higher absolute margin per booking. Day trips and port excursions — which range from $40 to $200 per person — operate at higher percentage margins of 40–60% because the variable cost structure is leaner (a licensed guide plus transport, no hotel component), though the absolute margin per booking is modest. Shore excursions sold through cruise ship partnerships or via TripAdvisor listings carry similar margin profiles. Nile cruises on standard cruise ships operate at the thinnest margins in the portfolio, typically 15–22%, because berth costs on popular routes are effectively fixed whether or not the cabin is sold, and competition among operators for the same inventory is intense.

## Discount and Promotion Policies

Memphis Tours operates a structured but consultant-administered discount framework rather than a public coupon or promotional code system. This is a deliberate commercial choice: it preserves pricing integrity on comparison platforms while allowing flexibility in individual negotiations.

The early bird policy offers 5–10% off packages booked more than 90 days before the departure date. This discount incentivizes advance bookings, which improve planning accuracy for operations and reduce the risk of last-minute supplier cost increases. Last-minute deals — bookings within 14 days of travel — are managed opportunistically based on availability and are not advertised at a fixed percentage. A repeat client discount of 5–8% is applied at the consultant's discretion for returning customers; given that the client database holds over 866,000 records going back to 2010, identifying repeat clients is a systematic part of the CRM workflow. Group discounts scale from 5% for six travelers to as much as 15% for groups of twenty or more.

Seasonal promotional campaigns include Black Friday (increasingly important for LATAM markets), Christmas and New Year promotions (timed to the January booking surge), and summer low-season promotions targeting off-peak Egypt cultural travel. Market-specific promotions are timed to cultural calendar events: Brazilian Carnival travel windows, Italian Ferragosto (the August holiday period), and the back-to-school booking surge that characterizes September in the French and Spanish markets. All of these promotions are executed by sales consultants applying approved discount parameters — there is no self-service booking engine where a customer can enter a code and see the discount applied automatically.

## Cancellation and Amendment Policies

For day trips and single-day excursions — the products most prominently listed on TripAdvisor, where cancellation policy is a public-facing trust signal — Memphis Tours offers free cancellation up to 24 hours before the service. This is a competitive necessity given that TripAdvisor's platform surfaces cancellation flexibility as a filter, and the company's 15,000+ reviews and 4.9 average rating depend in part on maintaining customer-friendly policies on these accessible products.

For multi-day packages, the cancellation policy follows a sliding scale calibrated to the sunk costs Memphis Tours incurs when unwinding a confirmed booking. Full refunds are available when cancellation occurs 60 or more days before departure. Between 30 and 59 days, the refund is 75% of the total paid, reflecting the partial recovery of deposits paid to hotels and ground operators. Between 15 and 29 days, the refund is 50%. Within 14 days of departure, no refund is issued, as at this point hotel bookings are non-refundable and guide and transport commitments are locked. These thresholds are policy defaults and can be adjusted in genuine exceptional circumstances — medical emergencies, political disruptions, or major force majeure events — subject to director approval.

Amendments to confirmed bookings are common; approximately 30% of bookings experience at least one change between confirmation and travel, ranging from date shifts and hotel upgrades to partial itinerary restructuring. Minor amendments — small date adjustments within the same season, room category changes of the same tier — are processed without charge as a goodwill gesture and to maintain service reputation. Major amendments that require re-quoting suppliers, rebooking flights, or restructuring a multi-destination itinerary may attract a rebooking fee, typically $50–$150 per file depending on complexity. The `refunded` and `refund_amount` fields in the requests table provide the data trail for all cancellation and partial refund activity, enabling finance to reconcile against bank records and operations to track the actual cost recovery from unwound supplier bookings.

## Payment Terms

The standard deposit at booking confirmation is 30% of the total package price. For peak season bookings (October–April Egypt cultural travel, June–September Red Sea), the deposit requirement may be raised to 40–50% on the advice of the reservations team, reflecting the higher opportunity cost of holding inventory in high-demand periods. The balance is due 30 days before the departure date. For bookings made within 30 days of travel, full payment is required at the time of confirmation — there is no split-payment option for late bookings.

Payment methods include credit card (the most common channel for individual travelers), bank wire transfer (preferred for high-value bookings above approximately $5,000 to avoid credit card processing fees, which typically run 2.5–3.5%), and PayPal (accepted in selected markets, particularly where card penetration is lower or consumer trust in PayPal is high). Payment lifecycle tracking in the database is managed through `payment_url_id` and `payment_url_case` fields on the requests table, which link to the payment gateway records and enable the finance team to track deposit receipt, balance due dates, and final settlement status.

## Group Size and Lead Time Requirements

Private tour products — the core of the Memphis Tours offering — have a minimum of one person, with the solo supplement applying as described above. Small group departures operate with a minimum of four travelers and a maximum of fifteen to twenty, depending on vehicle configuration and the specific destination (Egypt overland routes accommodate larger groups than, for example, a Dahabiya Nile sailing). Shore excursions require a minimum of two travelers. Corporate and large group travel has no published maximum; these bookings are handled by the operations team on a bespoke basis with custom logistics coordination.

Lead time requirements vary sharply by product complexity. Day trips and port excursions can be arranged with as little as 24–48 hours' notice, subject to guide and transport availability. Multi-day packages require a minimum of 14 days to confirm hotel availability across all cities in the itinerary. For peak season and complex itineraries (multi-country tours, multi-city Nile cruise combinations), 30–90 days of lead time is strongly recommended and should be communicated early in the sales consultation. Dahabiya Nile cruises — small, boutique sailing vessels with limited capacity — and luxury Nile steamer products can book out 6–12 months in advance during the October–April high season, making early booking not merely recommended but functionally necessary for clients with fixed travel dates.

## Regulatory and Compliance Framework

Memphis Tours holds Egyptian Tourism License No. 30, Category A — the highest classification issued by the Egyptian Ministry of Tourism and Antiquities. Category A designation is a formal prerequisite for operating inbound tours in Egypt and signals to both B2B partners and end consumers that the operator meets the government's highest standards for financial stability, professional conduct, and guide qualification. All guides working on Memphis Tours bookings hold licenses issued by the Egyptian Ministry of Tourism, a non-negotiable legal requirement. This licensing structure is a genuine competitive moat: smaller or newer operators cannot legally replicate it without going through the same licensing process.

Visa assistance is provided for most nationalities visiting Egypt (e-visa facilitation) and Jordan (visa on arrival information and coordination), though Memphis Tours is not a visa agent and does not guarantee visa outcomes. Travel insurance is strongly recommended but not included in package pricing; in certain European source markets — particularly Germany and France — regulations and consumer expectations create informal pressure to include or at minimum offer insurance as an add-on. The company is not a licensed insurance broker, so policies are referred rather than sold directly.

For European client data, GDPR compliance governs how client records are collected, stored, and processed — directly relevant given that Italy, France, Germany, Spain, and the UK collectively represent a significant share of the client base. For the Brazilian market (96,291 clients in the database), Brazil's LGPD (Lei Geral de Proteção de Dados) applies equivalent data governance requirements. Both regulatory frameworks require documented consent, data retention policies, and the ability to fulfill data deletion requests — areas managed by the marketing and IT teams in coordination with the company's legal advisors.

## Commercial Tensions and Known Friction Points

Several pricing and policy decisions create measurable conversion friction but exist for sound operational or financial reasons. The single supplement remains the most common point of client complaint and negotiation resistance — solo travelers, particularly in the growing solo female travel segment served by the She Drives brand, perceive it as a penalty for their travel style rather than a reflection of hotel economics. The tension is real and unlikely to be fully resolved without either building more shared-room group departure products or absorbing the supplement cost into base pricing (which compresses margins).

FX volatility is an ongoing commercial risk. When the EGP weakens against USD, the local cost base becomes more competitive, but when EUR or GBP weakens against USD, European revenue is eroded without relief from the cost side. The lack of real-time hedging means margin protection depends on timely rate adjustments, which can lag market moves by weeks.

Competitor price-matching pressure is concentrated in the budget and mid-range segment, where TripAdvisor and Google comparison shopping expose listed prices to direct comparison with smaller local operators who carry lower overhead. This creates a structural tension between the need to maintain competitive listed prices for SEO and discovery purposes, and the need to protect margins — a tension that the consultant-administered discount model partially manages but cannot fully resolve.

The 30% deposit requirement, while commercially sensible, is a documented friction point in the Latin American markets — particularly Brazil and Mexico, where pay-later and installment payment preferences are culturally embedded. Some price-sensitive LATAM customers abandon the booking process at the payment stage, and the deposit policy is a contributing factor. Similarly, the absence of instant confirmation for most products — a reflection of the custom, consultant-led booking process — creates a speed disadvantage compared to OTAs and automated booking platforms. This friction is an inherent cost of the tailor-made model, and one that Memphis Tours has accepted as consistent with its value proposition, but it remains a factor in conversion rate analysis.
